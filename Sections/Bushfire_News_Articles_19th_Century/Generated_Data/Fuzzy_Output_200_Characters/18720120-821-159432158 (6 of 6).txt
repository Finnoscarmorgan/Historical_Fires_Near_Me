Then the use of ignitable wadding in guns is inter dicted ; travellers camping out are required to clear a space round their camp ; and smoking in the open air within twenty yards of any stable, hayrick, or stubble-field is prohibited. All these restrictions have be come familiar to the public, and their wisdom is so generally acknowledged that proceedings under the Act have become ex ceedingly unfrequent. Of course no amount of legislation can prevent carelessness in the use of matches—the most fertile source of danger—but that the Bush Fires Act has been useful, and can be recommended as likely to be of advantage in the neighbour ing colony, we do not hesitate to say. The penalties provided are severe without being immoderate, and the provisions are suffi ciently elastic without being easily capable of evasion.
