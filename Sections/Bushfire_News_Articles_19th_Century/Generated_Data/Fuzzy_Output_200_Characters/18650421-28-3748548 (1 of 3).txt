BLACK MONDAY.---Monday, February 27,1865----hereafter to be styled " Black Monday" in the annals of Victoria---was a day which will not soon pass from the memory of those who endured its terrible weather. The sun rose sullenly, and its aspect threatened uncomfortable conse- quences. This was no deception ; for the day had scarcely set in when began a fierce sirocco wind from the north-north- west, exceeding in its wild velocity and overpowering heat the experience of the last fourteen years. It was felt in nearly equal degree all over the country. Awful bush-fires speedily commenced, and in Melbourne the heavy smoke filled the city like a thick fog. It brought this relief, that we had not the " furnace of the light" strike up against the " blind- ing walls." That, however, was a small mitigation of what we had to endure. The blasts, which came with the force of a hurricane, were as though they had left the mouth of an oven. The dust filled up every wrinkle of one's clothes and every pore of one's skin.
