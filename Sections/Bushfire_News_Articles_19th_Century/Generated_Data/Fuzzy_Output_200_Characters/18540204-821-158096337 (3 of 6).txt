All the necessary elements 5 of disaster are ready at hand; and probably in greater profusion than they were ever found in before. Tues- day last would give our newly-arrived friends a specimen of the sort of weather which we may occa sionally expect during the next two mouths- and upon any one such day, the disasters of Black Thurs day may be repeated. Few people that have not had some actual experi ence in such matters can thoroughly understand what the bush fire of Australia is, and how very greatly it differs in intensity under different circum- stances. When the country is open, the grass a little green and the air calm, the hush-fire creeps along slowly and quietly, aud may be kept under readily enough, when advancing towards valuable property. But in thick scrubs, or when the herbage is parched up, and the wind high and from the north, the fire spreads with fearful rapidity; it runs aiong the ground licking up the grass almost as if it were a train of gunpowder, and no earthly power can stay its progress.
